# CAMHS CBT Companion — Resource & Content Guide

This document serves as the content bible for the four core resource categories in the app: **Anxiety**, **Low Mood**, **Behavioural Challenges**, and **Sleep**. For each category it covers:

- A concise manual summary
- What the manual is trying to teach
- What generally happens in each session
- Learn resources (psychoeducation content)
- Activities and worksheets

Session numbers are indicative and used for suggested ordering only — no resource should be locked to a specific session.

# CATEGORY 1: ANXIETY

## Manual Summary

The anxiety CBT manual for young people is grounded in the well-established Cognitive Behavioural model, with strong roots in programmes like *Think Good Feel Good* (Stallard), *Coping Cat* (Kendall), and the NICE-recommended CYP IAPT high-intensity CBT pathway. It is typically delivered over **8–12 sessions** of 50–60 minutes, though a shorter 6–8 session version is also used in stepped-care contexts.

**What the manual is trying to teach:**

The core idea is that anxiety is maintained by a vicious cycle of unhelpful thoughts, physical feelings, and avoidance behaviours — and that breaking any part of that cycle creates change. Young people learn to:

- Understand anxiety as a normal biological response ("fight or flight") that sometimes gets stuck in the "on" position
- Notice what happens in their body, thoughts, and behaviour when they feel anxious
- Challenge and reframe unhelpful predictions and overestimates of danger
- Gradually face feared situations rather than avoid them (exposure)
- Build a toolkit of coping strategies they can use independently

A key principle throughout is that avoidance maintains anxiety. The exposure work — facing fears in a graded, manageable way — is the engine of change. Psychoeducation and cognitive work create the understanding that makes the exposure feel possible.

**What it looks like in practice:**

Sessions begin with psychoeducation, move through cognitive skills, build an individualised fear ladder, then work up the ladder using planned exposures. Later sessions consolidate learning and focus on relapse prevention.

---

## Session-by-Session Outline

### Session 1 — Understanding Anxiety
What this session covers: Introduction to the CBT model and the anxiety cycle. What is anxiety? Why does it happen? What does it feel like in the body? Meeting the "worry brain" and the "wise brain." Goal-setting. Brief introduction to monitoring.

### Session 2 — The Anxiety Cycle
What this session covers: The thoughts-feelings-behaviour triangle. Understanding how avoidance keeps anxiety alive. Introducing the concept of facing fears as the way through. Normalising anxiety. Completing a personal anxiety map.

### Session 3 — Body Clues and Relaxation
What this session covers: Identifying personal physical symptoms of anxiety (heart racing, stomach, muscles). Introduction to relaxation strategies including slow breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, and grounding. Practising one technique in session.

### Session 4 — Thought Challenging
What this session covers: Identifying automatic anxious thoughts. Common thinking traps (catastrophising, mind-reading, fortune-telling). The thought challenging process: What is the evidence? What would I tell a friend? What is a more balanced thought?

### Session 5 — Building the Fear Ladder
What this session covers: Creating a personalised fear hierarchy, also called a worry ladder or fear ladder. Rating feared situations by difficulty (0–10). Identifying realistic, achievable starting points. Understanding why we start small.

### Session 6 — Starting Exposure
What this session covers: Planning and beginning the first exposure steps. What to expect (anxiety goes up then comes down). The importance of staying in the situation until anxiety reduces. Reviewing first exposure attempts and problem-solving.

### Session 7 — Moving Up the Ladder
What this session covers: Working on higher-difficulty items from the fear hierarchy. Reviewing progress. Addressing setbacks without self-criticism. Introducing the idea that coping with some anxiety is the goal, not eliminating it completely.

### Session 8 — Coping Toolkit and Relapse Prevention
What this session covers: Building a personal coping toolkit. Reviewing what has worked. Identifying warning signs that anxiety may be returning. Planning for future difficult situations. Celebrating progress. Preparing for the end of sessions.

*For longer programmes (10–12 sessions), additional sessions are used to: address specific anxiety subtypes (social anxiety, health anxiety, panic); deepen exposure work; and include parent/carer sessions.*

# CATEGORY 2: LOW MOOD

## Manual Summary

The low mood CBT manual for young people is primarily based on **Behavioural Activation (BA)**, which is the most widely evidenced and accessible intervention for adolescent depression in CAMHS settings, alongside the broader CBT for depression model. Key sources include the *Brief Behavioural Activation for Adolescent Depression* manual (Pass & Reynolds, published 2020), the *ACTION* programme, and the standard high-intensity CBT for depression pathway within CYP IAPT.

Programmes typically run for **8–12 sessions**. Brief BA can be delivered in as few as 6–8 sessions.

**What the manual is trying to teach:**

Low mood and depression are understood through the lens of a gradual withdrawal from meaningful activity. When young people feel low, they tend to reduce what they do — especially things that previously gave them pleasure, connection, or a sense of achievement. This withdrawal makes mood worse, which leads to more withdrawal. Breaking this cycle through carefully planned activity is the engine of change.

Alongside this, young people learn to:

- Understand the connection between mood, thoughts, behaviour, and the body
- Recognise the withdrawal-low mood cycle
- Identify activities that are personally meaningful and likely to lift mood
- Plan and schedule activities in a graded, achievable way
- Notice and gently challenge unhelpful thoughts that maintain low mood (e.g. "there's no point", "nothing will help")
- Build future planning and relapse prevention skills

**What it looks like in practice:**

Sessions begin with psychoeducation and mood monitoring. The middle phase focuses on helping the young person identify what has dropped out of their life, link this to mood, and begin reintroducing valued and enjoyable activities in small steps. Cognitive work is threaded through, and later sessions focus on consolidating skills and planning for the future.

---

## Session-by-Session Outline

### Session 1 — Understanding Low Mood
What this session covers: What is depression or low mood? How does it differ from ordinary sadness? The low mood cycle. Introduction to mood monitoring. Goal setting. What to expect from sessions.

### Session 2 — The Mood and Activity Link
What this session covers: Introducing the core behavioural activation idea: how what we do affects how we feel. Completing an activity diary to see patterns. Identifying what has been dropped or reduced. Noticing the link between activities and mood ratings.

### Session 3 — Finding What Matters
What this session covers: Identifying personally meaningful activities — things that previously gave enjoyment, connection, achievement, or purpose. Thinking about values: what matters to this young person? Building an activity menu for the weeks ahead.

### Session 4 — Planning and Scheduling Activity
What this session covers: Learning how to plan small, achievable activity steps. Breaking things down so they feel manageable. Scheduling activities in advance. Understanding that motivation often follows action (not the other way around).

### Session 5 — Reviewing and Problem-Solving
What this session covers: Looking at how planned activities went. Celebrating any progress. Troubleshooting barriers: what got in the way? Adjusting plans. Addressing the "what's the point" thought pattern.

### Session 6 — Thought Patterns in Low Mood
What this session covers: Identifying common thinking patterns that come with low mood (self-criticism, hopelessness, all-or-nothing thinking). Gentle thought challenging using evidence-gathering and perspective-taking.

### Session 7 — Staying Connected
What this session covers: The role of social connection in mood. Barriers to connection when low. Graded steps to re-engage with others. Thinking about supportive vs. unhelpful relationships.

### Session 8 — Relapse Prevention and Future Planning
What this session covers: Identifying early warning signs that mood may be dropping. Building a personalised staying-well plan. Reviewing what has worked. Recognising progress. Planning support if things get hard again.

*For longer programmes, additional sessions may address: self-critical thinking in more depth; grief or loss if relevant; school and social challenges; family involvement.*

# CATEGORY 3: BEHAVIOURAL CHALLENGES

## Manual Summary

The behavioural challenges CBT manual is designed for young people experiencing difficulties with anger, impulsivity, conflict, and behaviour that is causing problems at home, school, or in relationships. It draws primarily on **Problem-Solving Skills Training (PSST)**, **Anger Management CBT**, and elements of Coping Power and Incredible Years programmes adapted for adolescents. In CAMHS, this pathway typically runs for **8–12 sessions**, sometimes with parallel parent sessions.

**What the manual is trying to teach:**

Young people with behavioural challenges often react quickly to perceived threat, frustration, or unfairness — and the behavioural response (aggression, defiance, withdrawal) tends to make the situation worse. The core of this work is helping young people to:

- Understand the thought-feeling-behaviour connection and recognise that their reactions make sense given their interpretations
- Identify their own early warning signs (physical and emotional) before behaviour escalates
- Learn to pause and slow down their reaction (stop and think)
- Build a repertoire of problem-solving strategies and communication skills
- Understand how their interpretation of situations affects their responses (e.g. hostile attribution bias — assuming others are being deliberate)
- Develop empathy and perspective-taking skills
- Practise applying these skills to real-life situations

**What it looks like in practice:**

Sessions move from understanding and awareness, through skills-building, to practising those skills in increasingly challenging situations. A strong emphasis is placed on making skills feel practical and personally relevant — not just theoretical. Role-play, scenario work, and structured problem-solving are all common.

---

## Session-by-Session Outline

### Session 1 — Understanding Behaviour and Feelings
What this session covers: Introduction to the CBT model in the context of behaviour. How thoughts and feelings lead to actions. Why behaviour that seems problematic often makes complete sense from the inside. Goal setting. Building rapport.

### Session 2 — My Warning Signs
What this session covers: Identifying personal early warning signs — what happens in the body and mind in the lead-up to an emotional outburst or difficult behaviour. Introducing the idea that noticing warning signs early creates more choice.

### Session 3 — Stop, Think, Choose
What this session covers: The stop-think-choose framework. Introducing a brief pause between trigger and reaction. Practising this in low-stakes situations. Understanding that the pause creates space for a different choice.

### Session 4 — Thinking About Thinking
What this session covers: How we interpret situations affects how we react. Introducing hostile attribution bias — the tendency to assume others are doing things on purpose. Exploring how the same situation can be read in different ways.

### Session 5 — Problem-Solving Skills
What this session covers: A structured problem-solving model (define the problem, generate options, evaluate options, choose one, try it, review). Practising with real examples from the young person's life.

### Session 6 — Managing Anger
What this session covers: Anger as a normal, valid emotion that becomes a problem when it drives harmful behaviour. Physiological calming strategies. Thinking about anger costs and benefits. Planning what to do when anger is building.

### Session 7 — Communication and Conflict
What this session covers: The difference between aggressive, passive, and assertive communication. Practising assertive communication. Introducing perspective-taking: how might the other person see this? Handling conflict constructively.

### Session 8 — Putting It Together and Planning Ahead
What this session covers: Reviewing all the skills covered. Applying them to the young person's most challenging real-life situations. Building a personal plan for difficult moments. Planning ahead for likely future challenges.

*For longer programmes, additional sessions may address: peer pressure and friendships; managing conflict at home; working with school; and, where included, parent sessions on consistent boundary-setting and positive reinforcement.*

# CATEGORY 4: SLEEP

## Manual Summary

The sleep CBT manual for young people is based on **Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)**, adapted for adolescents. CBT-I is the first-line recommended treatment for insomnia in adults and has good evidence in young people. Key sources include the adolescent CBT-I literature (including work by Gradisar and Harvey), the adapted brief CBT-I protocol described by Pass and Reynolds, and NICE-endorsed digital CBT-I approaches for young people.

Sleep problems are particularly common in young people attending CAMHS — up to a third report significant insomnia — and addressing sleep often has benefits for anxiety and mood as well. Programmes typically run for **6–8 sessions**, though content can be delivered across fewer sessions in a guided self-help format.

**What the manual is trying to teach:**

Sleep problems in young people are usually maintained by a combination of unhelpful sleep habits, physiological hyperarousal at bedtime, and inaccurate or anxious beliefs about sleep. The manual teaches young people to:

- Understand how sleep works (sleep drive, circadian rhythm, the role of the body clock)
- Recognise how their current behaviours (late screens, variable wake times, lying in bed awake) are maintaining the problem
- Strengthen the association between bed and sleep (stimulus control)
- Regulate their sleep schedule to build homeostatic sleep drive
- Challenge catastrophic or anxious thoughts about sleep (e.g. "if I don't sleep I'll fail everything")
- Use relaxation and wind-down strategies effectively
- Manage the common adolescent tendency toward a delayed body clock

**What it looks like in practice:**

Sessions begin with psychoeducation and sleep diary monitoring. The middle phase focuses on sleep scheduling, stimulus control, and gradual behavioural change. Cognitive work on sleep-related worry and catastrophising is woven through. Later sessions focus on maintaining gains and planning ahead.

---

## Session-by-Session Outline

### Session 1 — Understanding Sleep
What this session covers: How sleep works: the two-process model (sleep drive and body clock). Sleep stages. How much sleep young people need. Common myths about sleep. Introduction to sleep diary monitoring.

### Session 2 — What Is Keeping You Awake?
What this session covers: Reviewing the sleep diary. Identifying the key factors maintaining the sleep problem: irregular schedule, long time in bed, screen use, daytime napping, stimulating pre-bed activities, worry. Understanding which factors apply to this young person.

### Session 3 — Sleep Hygiene and Environment
What this session covers: What sleep hygiene is and is not. Key environmental factors: light, temperature, sound. Reviewing the young person's bedroom environment. Screens and blue light: the evidence and practical strategies. Building a pre-bed wind-down routine.

### Session 4 — Stimulus Control
What this session covers: The principle of stimulus control: bed should be associated with sleep, not wakefulness. What to do when you cannot sleep. Getting up vs. staying in bed. Addressing the fear that this will make things worse initially.

### Session 5 — Sleep Scheduling
What this session covers: Setting a consistent wake time. Compressing time in bed to build sleep drive. Managing the adolescent delayed sleep phase. Planning for school days vs. weekends. Understanding why consistency matters more than total hours.

### Session 6 — Managing Sleep Worry and Thoughts
What this session covers: Identifying catastrophic beliefs about sleep and their consequences. Common sleep thought traps: overestimating the impact of bad nights, all-or-nothing thinking about sleep. Gentle thought challenging for sleep-related worry. Worry time and constructive worry management.

### Session 7 — Relaxation and Wind-Down
What this session covers: Relaxation techniques that work for sleep: slow breathing, body scan, progressive muscle relaxation, imagery. Building an individualised wind-down routine. Mindful acceptance of wakefulness.

### Session 8 — Staying Well and Reviewing Progress
What this session covers: Reviewing what has changed. Sleep diary comparison from start to now. Identifying what has helped most. Building a sleep maintenance plan. Planning for setbacks (illness, exams, holidays). Ending sessions.

# Notes for Content Management

**Adding resources to the app**

When uploading resources, set the following for each entry:
- Category: Anxiety / Low Mood / Behavioural Challenges / Sleep
- Section: Learn or Activities
- Typical session number: use the numbers above as a guide
- Visibility: Young person / Parent / Practitioner — see role notes in each section
- Export type: Blank template (for worksheets) / Structured summary (for review outputs) / None (for Learn content)
- Status: Start as Draft until reviewed and ready

**Visibility notes**

Most Learn resources are visible to both young people and parents. A small number are practitioner-facing (e.g. detailed clinical rationale). Activities are primarily for young people. Parent-specific resources are clearly labelled.

**Content to prioritise at launch**

For a working V1, prioritise:
- All Session 1–3 Learn resources across all four categories (these are needed earliest in any young person's journey)
- Core activity worksheets: My Anxiety Map, Thought Challenging Record, My Fear Ladder, My Mood Diary, Activity Planner, My Warning Signs Map, Sleep Diary
- At least one parent-facing resource per category

**Content to add post-launch**

- Practitioner-specific rationale documents
- Deeper resources for sessions 6–8
- Specific topic deep-dives (e.g. social anxiety, panic, school-related stress)
- Additional parent guides
